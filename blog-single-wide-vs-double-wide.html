Single-Wide vs. Double-Wide: Which Fits Your Family?

A clear, no-pressure comparison to help you pick the right size home for your family and your land.

Buyer's Guide

One of the first choices you will make is the size of your home, and it usually comes down to single-wide or double-wide. Neither is better than the other. They just fit different families, different land, and different budgets. Here is a friendly side-by-side so you can figure out which one feels right for you, without any of the pressure.

What the terms actually mean

The names describe how the home is built and delivered. A single-wide is one long section that arrives in a single piece, so it is narrower and cozier. A double-wide is built in two sections that are joined together on your site to create one wider home. There are even larger multi-section homes for bigger families. It really is that simple.

Single-wide: cozy, efficient, and budget-friendly

A single-wide is a wonderful fit for a lot of people. Because there is less house, it usually costs less, heats and cools efficiently, and fits comfortably on a smaller or narrower lot. It is a favorite for first-time buyers, couples, smaller families, and anyone who wants a brand-new home without a big footprint.

  • Great for: individuals, couples, small families, tighter budgets, smaller lots.
  • Strengths: lower price, lower utility bills, easier to place, quick to set up.
  • Worth knowing: rooms and hallways are narrower, and storage is more modest.

Our Clayton TRU line has some excellent single-section homes that feel bright and modern inside. You can browse them in our current inventory.

Double-wide: room to spread out

A double-wide gives you a floor plan that feels a lot like a traditional site-built house. With the extra width you get bigger bedrooms, open living and kitchen areas, more bathrooms, and real storage. For growing families or anyone who loves to host, that extra space changes daily life.

  • Great for: growing families, folks who want a home-office or guest room, anyone who entertains.
  • Strengths: spacious open layouts, more bedrooms and baths, a more traditional feel.
  • Worth knowing: it costs more than a single-wide and needs a bit more land and site prep.

Clayton and Scotbilt both build beautiful multi-section homes. Take a look at the Clayton floor plans and Scotbilt floor plans to see the difference in person on paper.

A quick way to decide: start with how many bedrooms and bathrooms your family truly needs, then look at your land and your budget. Nine times out of ten, that points clearly to one or the other, and we are happy to help you think it through.

Four questions that usually settle it

  1. How many people will live here, now and in a few years? More people or a growing family leans double-wide.
  2. How much land do you have? A smaller or narrower lot may be perfect for a single-wide.
  3. What monthly payment feels comfortable? A single-wide keeps the number lower. See our guide on how financing works.
  4. How do you use your home? If you host, work from home, or want a guest room, the extra space of a double-wide earns its keep.
